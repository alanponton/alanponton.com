Recruiter Call

Stage 1 · 30–45 min · Teams or phone
Tell me about yourself (90 sec)

Thanks for the opportunity. I've been looking forward to this because American's digital products reach millions of travelers, and that's the scale I want to design for. In one sentence, I'm a designer who ships: I design in Figma and build the production code myself, so nothing gets lost between design intent and what users actually touch. I bring about twenty years of technical experience, fourteen of them in enterprise IT for the State of New Jersey, where I moved from help desk to IT Specialist 3, essentially the network administrator role, and led my department's side of an email migration for a 6,600-employee agency. My recent work includes 2100 Security, a scheduling app five officers use nightly on their phones, and Lonnie's Locations, a group travel platform live in production with real guests. Bring me on, and you get someone who solves real problems for real users and wants a long-term career doing it here.

Scale → Ships → New Jersey → Two products → Long-term
Why American Airlines?

Three honest reasons. I'm local, so headquarters is home turf, no relocation, no hesitation. The scale: your digital products touch millions of travelers, and I want my decisions living at that scale. And I've applied to American before, going back a few years. I've wanted to be here a while. The difference now is my portfolio finally matches the ambition.

Experian, PM intent into code → New Jersey → Texas and 2100 → Certs, consulting, next step

That's a genuine growth area, and one reason this team appeals to me. My research so far has been direct: watching my own users in real conditions and reversing my designs when they failed. What I want next is the formal side, moderated testing and structured research, inside a team like yours.

Behavioral STAR

Under 90 sec each
A time you got something wrong (primary)

Version one of 2100 Security looked great. Dark landing page, glowing accents, real security-tech polish, and I was proud of it. Then my officers told me they couldn't read it on a long overnight shift. I'd designed for the user in my head instead of a tired person at 4 a.m. in a dark stairwell. So in version two I stripped my own work back: clean palette, high contrast, readability over aesthetics. The lesson stuck: my taste is not the user's eyesight.

Catching a serious mistake (backup)

In version two of 2100, guard names and floor assignments updated separately, and mid-build I caught that they could drift apart, meaning an officer sent to the wrong post. On a security schedule that's not cosmetic. I'd architected it wrong, so I rebuilt the data so floors travel with the guard and can't decouple. Now I validate multi-constraint systems one change at a time: change one thing, verify, then the next.

Handling pushback

I defend design decisions with rationale, never preference. When my security manager said he couldn't read the schedule on shift, I didn't defend the design, I added dark mode, because his constraint was real. When I believe the design is right, I cite the best practice, the research, or the requirement behind it. If I can't cite a reason, I shouldn't be defending it.

Disagreeing with a manager (NJ, big-org story)

During our GroupWise-to-Exchange migration at the state, management requested an authorized email investigation. The standard method was logging into the user's PC directly. The user had already been migrated, and I knew Exchange gave us a cleaner, seamless way, no touching the machine. My supervisor didn't have confidence it would work. Instead of arguing, I set up a call between my supervisor, myself, and the DHS technician who'd trained me on the migration. The tech performed the new method live on the call, confirmed I had full access to do this for every user in our system, and followed up with an email to our management team. It became our standard method. What I learned: when your word isn't enough, don't argue harder, borrow credibility. A live demonstration ended a debate words never would have. Make it easy for the skeptic to say yes.

New way → supervisor doubted → live demo call → borrow credibility, don't argue

AI Questions

Three stories + judgment map
Story 1: When AI got it wrong (flagship)

My portfolio has an AI concierge I built, trained on a knowledge base about my projects. During QA I asked it about one of my projects, and the answer sounded great, and was fabricated. It invented user numbers I never gave it and borrowed the tech stack from a different project's entry. I traced the pattern: the model was filling gaps by bleeding context between knowledge base entries. So I engineered the fix at the system level: each project's entry is its own island, no borrowing across them, and if the answer isn't in the entry, it says "I don't have that detail" and offers to connect the visitor to me. The fabrication stopped, and it changed how I QA any AI system: I don't test whether answers sound right, I test whether they trace to source.

Concierge → invented details → island rule → trace to source
Story 2: When AI misreported its own work

I had my AI coding assistant wire thirteen image files to a data file and asked it to cross-check every filename. It told me it had cross-checked, all thirteen matched. Something felt off, so I ran the check myself in the shell. They didn't all match; the AI claimed a verification it never performed. Broken images never reached production, and I took a permanent rule: AI can do the work, but verification is mine. When correctness matters, the shell is the only ground truth. Same discipline as code review.

Thirteen files → "all matched" → ran it myself → verification is mine
Story 3: When AI was spot-on

The same concierge shows the other side. Solo, seven React components, a streaming protocol, and an edge function is weeks of work. With AI-augmented development I shipped the working end-to-end version in about a three-hour session, and my role stayed where it belongs: I made every architecture call, set the refusal boundaries and voice rules, reviewed every line, and caught two real bugs in testing before they shipped. AI compresses execution time. The decisions, the standards, and the accountability stay with me.

Whole feature → three hours → every line reviewed → decisions stay mine
"If AI does the work, why do we need you?"

Because AI has no stake in the outcome. It'll confidently ship the wrong thing, I've caught it doing exactly that. What I bring is the part AI can't do: knowing which problem is worth solving, what the user at 4 a.m. in a stairwell actually needs, and when the impressive-looking answer is wrong. AI compresses my execution time. It doesn't know my users, and it doesn't carry accountability. I do.

"Could you work without AI?"

Yes. I'm an intermediate coder who reads, modifies, and debugs; AI makes me faster, it's not a crutch. My design work is Figma, and design thinking doesn't need a tool at all. I'd feel the speed difference, honestly, but the quality bar wouldn't move, because the standards were never AI's to begin with.

"How do you use AI in design, not just code?"

Carefully, and never for decisions. I'll use it to pressure-test a flow by arguing against my own design, to draft interface copy variations I then edit, and to summarize my own notes. What I don't do is ask it what users want. That answer only comes from users.

"Have you designed FOR AI?"

Yes, my portfolio concierge is a designed AI experience, not just a feature. The interesting design problems were trust problems: what it refuses to discuss, how it admits "I don't have that detail" instead of guessing, and when it escalates to a human through a contact form. Designing the boundaries mattered more than designing the chat bubble. I think that's true of most AI products.

Canonical AI lines

AI accelerates my execution; it doesn't make my decisions. I ship only what I understand. I test whether it traces to source.

Portfolio & Process

Stage 2 · hiring manager round
2100 walkthrough, Beat 1: Problem (45 sec)

2100 Security is a progressive web app for the five-person overnight team I work on at a 33-floor Dallas high-rise. The old system was a free-tier site with no SSL, so every officer got a browser security warning, a security team whose own tool looked insecure. It was static, so I rebuilt the schedule by hand monthly, and on a phone it took pinch-zoom and horizontal scrolling to read an assignment. I lived this problem every night, which is exactly why I could design for it.

Beat 2: Users and constraints (60 sec)

My users are tired people, one-handed, mid-patrol, often in low light. I derived the constraints from watching officers use the old site in real conditions: dark by default, high contrast, large targets, never any horizontal scroll. The governing principle came from watching a chat-tool rollout die, not on features, on forgotten passwords. So identity is a guard picker: tap your name, one-time employee ID, under five seconds, no email, no app store. Zero-friction access beats feature richness.

Beat 3: The decisions (90 sec, where you're scored)

Two decisions I'll defend anywhere. First, the exception to my own principle: confirming a fire-extinguisher inspection requires deliberate ID re-entry, added friction on purpose, because a careless tap on a safety record is real-world harm. That's since grown into a full forcing function: on the first of each month the app locks on the fire-patrol screen until the guard validates with their ID. A missed safety patrol shouldn't be preventable by discipline alone; I made it structurally impossible. Second, the reversal. My V1 looked great, and my officers couldn't read it across a long shift. I'd designed for the user in my head instead of a tired person at 4 a.m. in a stairwell. V2 stripped my own work back: readability over aesthetics. My taste is not the user's eyesight.

Beat 4: System and results (60 sec)

Under it runs a deterministic constraint engine, an availability foundation, four rotation rules, and the monthly safety gate; it's run as many as seven rules, and I prune the set as the team's reality changes. Deliberately not AI: same input, same output, because a security schedule needs reliability over cleverness. Results: security warnings gone, monthly updates went from hand-rebuilding to a single data-array edit, and five officers use it nightly across Android and iOS with zero onboarding.

Lived problem → tired one-handed users → friction both ways → deterministic engine, real results
"What would you change about 2100?"

The auto-generation. I built it, it violated its own constraints in edge cases, and I made the reliability call: the engine validates and renders, I generate. Fixing the generator's availability and uniqueness checks is on my roadmap, but I'd make the same call again. Silent automation failure in a security context is worse than manual work.

Your five design principles

Five, all earned in production. Zero-friction access beats feature richness: I watched a chat rollout die on forgotten passwords, so my flagship's login is tap-your-name, under five seconds. Except where friction saves people: confirming a fire-safety inspection requires ID re-entry on purpose, because a careless tap there is real-world harm. My taste is not the user's eyesight: I reversed my own favorite design when officers couldn't read it on shift. Design for who the user is, not who I imagine: I once built an aspirational itinerary over a client's actual plans and made the correction a governing rule. And constraints are errors made impossible: the best design decision is the wrong path that no longer exists.

Zero friction → except where friction saves → not my eyesight → who the user is → errors made impossible
Design process (five words)

My process starts where the user actually is. I define the real constraints before designing, all of them. I design in Figma and build the working version myself, because real beats simulated. Then I watch it fail, and reverse when the user says so. Ship, measure outcomes.

Observe, constrain, build, test, reverse when the user says so
Double Diamond bridge (honest version)

I built my process independently and later recognized most of it in the Double Diamond. Discover, I go where the user is: my officers on shift, Lonnie's inbox as a qualitative dataset. Define, I turn what I find into hard constraints before designing anything. Deliver, I ship and keep watching, and reverse when users prove me wrong. The honest gap is the second diamond, Develop: solo work under-trains divergence, because exploring three directions costs triple when you're also the builder. My pattern has been commit fast and correct in production. That's exactly where a team makes me better, and it's a muscle I'm actively building with timed design-challenge drills.

Found it independently → Discover, Define, Deliver are mine → divergence is the honest gap → a team fixes that
UX vs Product Design

UX design is the user's side: the journey, the flow, how it feels to move through the thing, whether it's usable, readable, trustworthy. Product design is UX plus the business: it asks not just "does this work for the user" but "should this exist, what does it cost, what does it earn, what do we build first." In practice I work as a product designer: on Lonnie's platform every flow decision was also a conversion decision, and on ROSE the refusal boundaries protect the business the agent represents, not just the user's experience. UX is the craft; product design is the craft plus the judgment about where to spend it.

UX is the user's side → product adds should-it-exist and what-first → craft plus judgment
"Why a team after years solo?"

Because solo is my ceiling and I know it. Working alone taught me every part of the process, but it also means my blind spots ship. My best design moments came from other people, my officers telling me they couldn't read my beautiful V1. A team is that correction loop running constantly, plus researchers and engineers who go deeper than I can alone. I've proven I can ship by myself. Now I want to find out what I ship when I'm not.

Solo is my ceiling → blind spots ship → V1 correction → what I ship when I'm not alone
Critique, PMs, engineers

Critique: Gratefully, and I have receipts. The most valuable feedback I ever got was my own users telling me my best-looking work was unreadable, and my response was to rebuild it their way. I don't defend taste. Taste isn't an argument.

PMs: I've been on the receiving end of PM requirements since Experian in the nineties. I want the problem and the success measure from the PM, and I'll bring back the constraint map and the tradeoffs. My model for disagreement is the Exchange-migration story: borrow credibility, don't argue.

Engineers: I am one, part-time, which mostly means I never hand engineering a fantasy. My specs account for states, edge cases, and failure modes because I've had to build my own. I don't design what I wouldn't want to implement.

Screen-share mechanics

Share the browser window, never the desktop. One tab (alanponton.com), bookmarks bar hidden, navigate by clicking, not typing. Words lead, scroll follows: scroll, settle, then speak. Money stop: V1 next to V2 while saying "my taste is not the user's eyesight." Park the cursor.

2100 Constraints

Canonical v2 · foundation, four rules, one gate
Rule 0: Availability foundation

S14Sun through Thu
S15Tue through Sat
S16Wednesday only

A guard is only ever scheduled on contracted work days. Every other rule operates inside this one.

The four active rotation rules

1. No consecutive-post repeats: never the same post on back-to-back working nights. 2. No duplicate floor ranges: never the same floor range twice in one week. 3. Soft landing: returning from two days off starts in North or Rover, never Command. 4. Clockwise rotation: Command to North to Rover; reversal only when forced by another rule (soft rule).

Retired, never claim as current: S16 Wednesday North anchor, S3 Friday Command weekend rule, weekly ground-floor coverage.
The Fire Gate (forcing function)

First of every month, the app holds every guard on the Fire Extinguisher screen until they validate completion with their employee ID; only then does the schedule return. A forcing function: a blocking gate that makes skipping a safety-critical step impossible rather than merely discouraged. Rotation rules constrain the schedule; the fire gate constrains the workflow. Same philosophy, two layers: constraints are errors made impossible in advance.

20-second spoken version

An availability foundation, four rotation rules, two never-repeat, two flow, and a monthly safety gate that locks the app on the fire-extinguisher screen until the guard validates with their ID. It's run as many as seven rules; I prune the set as the team's reality changes.

Foundation, four rules, one gate
The pruning story

The rule set is a living thing. Three rules existed because of specific staffing circumstances, and when those changed, I removed them instead of letting dead rules complicate the engine. Maintaining a constraint system means deleting constraints too. Requirements are versioned, not carved.

Numbers discipline + never say

Resume says "seven," site says "5–7": both describe rotations the engine actually generated, historically true. Spoken answers use current truth: five core rules plus a monthly gate; it's run as many as seven.

Never say: the schedule updates itself, or that the engine is AI. Deterministic solver, human-in-the-loop, by design. No "yet."

ROSE & Evolution

Deploys when the AI door opens · never leads
90-second ROSE walkthrough

ROSE is a white-label AI front-desk agent I designed and built solo. The problem: small businesses lose bookings to every unanswered question, and hiring front-desk coverage doesn't scale. A five-question setup wizard builds a branded agent in about sixty seconds. The agent handles customer questions in the business's voice and captures every booking request into a leads dashboard with an email notification to the owner. Under the hood it's Next.js 14 serverless on Vercel, Anthropic Claude with tool calling, and Supabase. Tool calling matters: the agent takes structured actions, every captured request lands as structured data while ROSE stays conversational. Because it's a public unauthenticated endpoint, I designed a two-gate abuse front door: bot challenge plus rate limiting. It's my fourth build of the same design problem, conversational intake, and it's live in production, developer-verified end to end, and honestly pre-revenue. I don't claim users I don't have.

Problem → 60-second wizard → tool calling → two-gate door → fourth build, honest status
The evolution arc (maturity, not chronology)

Four builds of the same design problem, conversational intake, each one teaching the next. Telegram bot (Aug 2025): architected, deliberately abandoned, never deployed; I recognized over-engineering and killed it. Ask Alan AI: first shipped conversational agent, live on alanponton.com. Sophia: reusable voice platform after two products needed the same foundation. ROSE: the pattern productized.

Killed it → Shipped it → Voiced it → Productized it
Weld to every Telegram mention: never deployed. Frame by product maturity; dates overlap, say so if pressed.
"Isn't this just a GPT wrapper?"

The model is a commodity; the product is everything around it. The 60-second wizard is an onboarding design problem. Tool calling turns conversation into structured leads. The two-gate abuse door is security design for a public endpoint. And the refusal and grounding work is what keeps it from embarrassing the business it represents. That's the design work, the model is one ingredient.

"It has no users. Why show it?"

Because it's live, end-to-end verified, and every claim about it is true. I'd rather show a real pre-revenue product honestly than inflate numbers. And it's the fourth generation of a design problem I've been iterating on, the evolution is the evidence.

Bespoke vs productized (signature)

Deliberate choice. Lonnie's concierge is bespoke, built into that product's data, refund rules, and voice, because a travel platform's failure modes are specific. ROSE is productized, generic by design so any business can wear it. Bespoke buys depth, productized buys reach, choosing between them is the judgment. Retrofitting one into the other would have been the wrong call in both directions.

Bespoke buys depth, productized buys reach, choosing is the judgment
2100 vs ROSE on AI (no blur)

2100's scheduling engine is deterministic on purpose, reliability over automation. ROSE is where I do use AI, because conversational intake is exactly what language models are for. Right tool, right problem.

The 429 mobile bug (diagnostic story)

The concierge worked on desktop and failed on every mobile attempt. I ran the diagnostic path: CORS recon found and fixed a real latent allowlist gap, not the bug; incognito ruled out cache; debug instrumentation surfaced HTTP 429. Root cause: my own lifetime 40-message session cap, my phone had exhausted its jar from weeks of testing. Fix: silent session rotation with one retry. Then the honest follow-through: I realized the cap never actually protected against abuse, so I shipped real per-IP rate limiting, hashed IPs, fails open so infrastructure hiccups never block real visitors. Full diagnostic discipline, plus discovering my own fix exposed a deeper design gap.

Non-negotiable: pre-revenue, developer-verified only, solo build, no user or traction claims. Tavi is an in-progress TEST until it actually ships. Whiteboard Challenge

FigJam · timed runs
The six frames (your FigJam template)

1. Understand your goal, question everything. Capture motive, user, current state, success. Close with the escape hatch: "Based on your data, I'm focusing on X. I'll assume mobile-first and that staff can be part of the solution. If that's wrong, tell me and I'll adjust." Data to decision, gap to assumption.

2. Define your audience, one person first. Name, one-line bio, THE MOMENT: day what, standing where, feeling what. And the disqualifier: who I'm NOT designing for. Everyone else is secondary.

3. Customer context and needs, two or three pains max. Written as scenes, the person's name on every sticky. Cut what the design can't touch. End with the bridge sentence: both pains fused in one line.

4. Three ideas, choose one, say what you're giving up. Three angles: cheap/fast, ambitious, in-between. Rejected ideas stay on the board, two words each. Then the commit line: "I'm choosing ___, it costs ___, but it protects ___." No hedging.

5. Sketch the core flow, three screens max, narrate decisions not pixels. Sketch FROM the pain stickies, not from memory. Lead with THEIR moment, not the company's fact. Screen zones: top answers "what is this," middle answers "am I okay," bottom answers "now what." Microcopy is design. Two screens is enough.

6. Measure success. One behavioral metric, one signal you were wrong: "I'd know this works if ___, and I'd know I was wrong if ___."

Right problem → right person → real pain → honest trade-off → shipped answer → proof
The three fouls

Google foul: researching the real app mid-challenge. Hedge foul: bolting a rejected idea onto the chosen one. Familiar-screen foul: sketching the pain point in Frame 5 instead of the solution.

Seven-step live skeleton

1. Restate and confirm. 2. Users: who, and what state (a stranded traveler is stressed, offline-prone, one-handed). 3. Constraints, name them before designing, out loud. 4. Prioritize the one moment that matters most, say why. 5. Sketch the flow, talk while drawing, ugly is fine. 6. Edge cases and failure modes: what breaks, what's the fallback. 7. Success measures and what you'd test first.

Your differentiator line (say during step 3)

I map constraints before designing because I've watched unconstrained designs collapse in production. My scheduling app runs on an availability foundation with rotation rules and a safety gate; mapping them first is why it works.

Take-home rules

Respect the timebox they give. Show two to three explored directions with reasons for the rejected ones, this trains the divergence gap on purpose. Present decisions, not screens. AA's documented example: redesign the mobile flow for a canceled connection with hotel and meal vouchers, presented to UX leads.

Panel: how to win each seat

PM wants outcomes and tradeoffs: lead with the problem and the decision. Engineer wants feasibility literacy: your easiest seat, let them discover you speak their language. Design lead wants process and taste: the V1 reversal and constraint mapping. Research wants honesty about methods: growth-area answer, never bluff this seat. Answer to the asker, sweep the room on the landing. Under 90 seconds. If two panelists disagree, never pick a side, name the tradeoff they're debating.

Stakeholder / difficult feedback spiel

My stakeholders have been clients and my own management. The pattern I've learned: bring them the decision with its reasoning, not just the artifact. When my security manager couldn't read the schedule on shift, the answer wasn't defending my design, it was dark mode, because his constraint was real. Clients are the same: Lonnie didn't need my aesthetic preferences, she needed guests booking without confusion. Rationale in, ego out.

Decision with reasoning → manager's dark mode → Lonnie's guests → rationale in, ego out
Offer stage rules

Never accept or negotiate on the call: "I'm excited, send it over and give me a couple of days to review properly." Level determines everything; negotiate within the band offered. Negotiate once, completely, in a single counter. Flight privileges are part of total comp; factor them, don't lead with them.

Thank-you formula

Within 24 hours, three sentences: one specific thing from that conversation, one line connecting it to what you'd bring, portfolio link. After a panel, individual notes referencing each person's own question. Specific beats polished.
