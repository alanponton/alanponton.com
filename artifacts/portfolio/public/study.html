Behavioral STAR

Under 90 sec each
A time you got something wrong (primary)

Version one of 2100 Security looked great. Dark screens, glowing accents, that high-tech security look, and I was proud of it. Then my guards told me they couldn't read it during a long overnight shift.

I had designed for the user in my head instead of a tired person at 4 a.m. in a dark stairwell. So in version two I stripped my own work back: cleaner colors, stronger contrast, readability over looks.

The lesson stuck: my taste is not the user's eyesight.

Catching a serious mistake (backup)

In version two of 2100, guard names and floor assignments were stored separately, and mid-build I caught that they could drift apart. That means a guard could get sent to the wrong post, and on a security schedule that's not a small bug.

I had built it wrong, so I rebuilt it: the floors now travel with the guard and can't come apart.

Since then I test systems like that one change at a time. Change one thing, check it, then the next.

Handling pushback

I defend design decisions with reasons, never with taste. When my manager said she couldn't read the schedule on shift, I didn't argue, I added dark mode, because her problem was real.

When I believe the design is right, I point to the reason behind it: the best practice, the feedback, or the requirement.

If I can't name a reason, I shouldn't be defending it.

Disagreeing with a manager (NJ, big-company story)

During our email migration at the state, management requested an approved email investigation. The old way was logging into the person's computer directly. But that account had already moved to the new system, and I knew a cleaner way that didn't touch their machine at all.

My supervisor didn't believe it would work. Instead of arguing, I set up a call with him, me, and the state technician who had trained me on the migration. The tech ran the new method live on that call, confirmed I had access to do it for anyone in our system, and emailed our management to back it up.

It became our standard way. What I learned: when your word isn't enough, don't argue harder, borrow credibility. A live demonstration ended a debate that words never would have.

New way → supervisor doubted → live demo call → borrow credibility, don't argue

AI Questions

Three stories + judgment answers
Story 1: When AI got it wrong (flagship)

My portfolio has an AI assistant I built. It answers visitor questions using a knowledge base I wrote about my projects. While testing it, I asked about one of my projects, and the answer sounded great, and was made up. It invented numbers I never gave it and mixed in details from a different project.

I traced the pattern: the AI was filling gaps by borrowing from other entries. So I fixed it at the system level. Each project's information is now its own island, no borrowing, and if the answer isn't there, the assistant says "I don't have that detail" and offers to connect the visitor with me.

The made-up answers stopped, and it changed how I test any AI. I don't check whether an answer sounds right. I check whether it can be traced back to the source.

Assistant → made-up details → island rule → trace to source
Story 2: When AI misreported its own work

I asked my AI coding assistant to connect thirteen image files to a data file, and to double-check that every filename matched. It told me it checked, and that all thirteen matched.

Something felt off, so I checked it myself, directly in the files. They didn't all match. The AI claimed it did a check it never did.

Broken images never reached the live site, and I took away a permanent rule: AI can do the work, but checking it is my job. Confidence isn't proof.

Thirteen files → "all matched" → checked it myself → checking is my job
Story 3: When AI was spot-on

The same assistant shows the other side. Building it alone would normally take me weeks: seven components, live streaming answers, a server function.

With AI helping me code, the working version was live in about three hours. My role stayed where it belongs: I made every design and structure decision, set the rules for what it can and can't say, read every line, and caught two real bugs before launch.

AI makes me faster. The decisions and the responsibility stay with me.

Whole feature → three hours → every line read → decisions stay mine
"If AI does the work, why do we need you?"

Because AI doesn't care if it's wrong. It will confidently build the wrong thing, I've caught it doing exactly that.

What I bring is the part AI can't: knowing which problem matters, what a tired guard at 4 a.m. actually needs, and when the impressive answer is wrong.

AI makes me faster. It doesn't know my users, and it doesn't take responsibility. I do.

"Could you work without AI?"

Yes. I can read, write, and fix code myself; AI just makes me faster. And design thinking doesn't need any tool at all.

I'd feel the speed difference, honestly. But the quality wouldn't drop, because the standards were never AI's. They're mine.

"How do you use AI in design, not just code?"

Carefully, and never for decisions. I'll use it to argue against my own design and see if it holds, to draft versions of screen text that I then edit, and to organize my notes.

What I never do is ask it what users want. That answer only comes from users.

"Have you designed FOR AI?"

Yes. My portfolio assistant is a designed AI experience, not just a feature.

The hard design problems were trust problems: what it refuses to talk about, how it says "I don't have that detail" instead of guessing, and when it hands the visitor to a real person, me.

Designing the limits mattered more than designing the chat window. I think that's true for most AI products.

Canonical AI lines

AI makes me faster; it doesn't make my decisions. I ship only what I understand. I check that it traces to the source.

Portfolio & Process

Stage 2 · hiring manager round
2100 walkthrough, Beat 1: Problem (45 sec, manager gets her props)

2100 Security is a web app for the five-person overnight security team I work on at a 33-floor Dallas high-rise. The schedule started as a sticky note taped to the key box, then an Excel sheet you had to zoom in to read on a phone.

My security manager saw the problem the same way I did. She approved the first version, a simple website, and when guards kept getting browser security warnings just opening it, she gave me the room to build something real. That backing is why this tool exists; the rest of the story is me earning it.

The old site couldn't update itself, so I rebuilt the schedule by hand every month. On a phone you had to pinch, zoom, and scroll sideways just to read your assignment. I lived this problem every night, which is exactly why I could design for it.

Practice rule: her pronoun is she. If it slips mid-run, say the sentence again correctly and keep going. No apology, no restart.
Beat 2: Users and constraints (60 sec)

My users are tired people, working one-handed, often in low light. I got my design rules from watching the guards use the old site in real conditions: dark screens by default, strong contrast, big buttons, and never any sideways scrolling.

The biggest rule came from watching a chat tool fail on our team, not because it lacked features, but because people forgot their passwords.

So there's no login form. You tap your name, enter your employee ID once, and you're in. Under five seconds, no email, no app store. Easy access beats extra features.

Beat 3: The decisions (90 sec, where you're scored)

Two decisions I'll defend anywhere. First, the exception to my own rule: confirming a fire-extinguisher inspection means typing your employee ID again, on purpose. A careless tap on a safety record is real-world harm, so I added friction exactly there.

That grew into something bigger. On the first of each month, the app locks on the fire-patrol screen until the guard confirms with their ID. A missed safety patrol shouldn't depend on memory or discipline. I made it impossible to skip.

Second, the reversal. My first version looked great, and my guards couldn't read it on a long shift. I had designed for the user in my head, not a tired person at 4 a.m. in a stairwell.

Version two stripped my own work back: readability over looks. My taste is not the user's eyesight.

Beat 4: System and results (60 sec)

Underneath runs a scheduling engine built on fixed rules: same input, same answer, every time. That's on purpose, not AI, because a security schedule needs to be dependable, not clever.

Today it runs on an availability foundation, four rotation rules, and the monthly safety gate. It's run as many as seven rules, and I remove rules when the team's reality changes.

The results: the security warnings are gone, the monthly update went from rebuilding a page by hand to editing one line of data, and five guards use it every night on Android and iPhone, with no training needed.

Lived problem → tired one-handed users → friction both ways → fixed rules, real results
"What would you change about 2100?"

The auto-scheduling. I built it, and in rare cases it broke its own rules: double-booked patrols, scheduled guards on their days off.

So I made the reliability call. The engine runs the nightly rotation and displays the schedule, and I generate it. Fifteen minutes a month with zero errors beats automation I can't trust.

Fixing it is on my list, but I'd make the same call again. In security, a quiet failure is worse than manual work.

Your five design principles

Five, all earned in production. Zero-friction access beats feature richness: I watched a chat tool die on forgotten passwords, so my app's login is tap-your-name, under five seconds.

Except where friction saves people: confirming a fire-safety inspection requires typing your ID again, on purpose, because a careless tap there is real-world harm.

My taste is not the user's eyesight: I reversed my own favorite design when the guards couldn't read it on shift.

Design for who the user is, not who I imagine: I once built a dream itinerary over a client's actual plans, and made the correction a permanent rule.

And constraints are errors made impossible: the best design decision is the wrong path that no longer exists.

Zero friction → except where friction saves → not my eyesight → who the user is → errors made impossible

My process starts where the user actually is. I list the real limits before designing, all of them.

I design in Figma and build the working version myself, because real beats simulated. Then I watch it fail, and change course when the user says so.

Ship, then measure what happened.

Double Diamond bridge (honest version)

I built my process on my own, and later found that most of it matches a known framework, the Double Diamond.

Discover: I go where the user is, my guards on shift, my client's inbox full of real customer questions. Define: I turn what I find into hard rules before designing anything. Deliver: I ship, keep watching, and change course when users prove me wrong.

The honest gap is the Develop stage: exploring several directions before committing. Working alone, exploring three directions costs triple, so my habit has been commit fast and correct in production.

That's exactly where a team makes me better, and I'm training that muscle now with timed design exercises.

UX vs Product Design

UX design is the user's side: the journey through the product, how it feels, whether it's easy, readable, and trustworthy.

Product design is UX plus the business side. Not just "does this work for the user," but "should we build this, what does it cost, what comes first."

In practice I work as a product designer. On Lonnie's travel site, every design choice was also a booking choice. On ROSE, the rules about what the AI won't say protect the business, not just the user.

UX is the craft. Product design is the craft plus deciding where to spend it.

UX is the user's side → product adds should-we-build-it → craft plus judgment

Because working alone has a ceiling, and I know it. Solo work taught me every part of the process, but it also means my blind spots go live.

My best design moments came from other people, like my guards telling me my beautiful first version was unreadable. A team is that correction running constantly, plus researchers and engineers who go deeper than I can alone.

I've proven I can ship by myself. Now I want to find out what I ship when I'm not.

Critique, PMs, engineers

Critique: Gratefully, and I have proof. The most valuable feedback I ever got was my own users telling me my best-looking work was unreadable, and I rebuilt it their way. I don't defend taste. Taste isn't an argument.

PMs: I've been building what product managers ask for since my first job in the nineties. I want the problem and the goal from them; I bring back the plan, the limits, and the trade-offs. The four active rotation rules

1. No repeat posts: never the same post two working nights in a row. 2. No repeat floors: never the same floor range twice in one week.

3. Soft landing: coming back from two days off, you start in North or Rover, never Command. 4. Clockwise rotation: posts move Command to North to Rover; going backward is allowed only when another rule forces it.

The Fire Gate (forcing function)

On the first of every month, the app holds every guard on the Fire Extinguisher screen until they confirm completion with their employee ID. Only then does the schedule come back.

Designers call this a forcing function: a gate that makes skipping a safety step impossible, instead of just discouraged.

The rotation rules protect the schedule. The fire gate protects the workflow. Same idea, two layers: mistakes made impossible in advance.

ROSE & Evolution

Deploys when the AI door opens · never leads
90-second ROSE walkthrough

ROSE is an AI front-desk assistant I designed and built alone, for small businesses. The problem: a small business loses bookings every time a question goes unanswered, and hiring someone to answer around the clock doesn't scale.

With ROSE, a five-question setup builds a branded assistant in about sixty seconds. It answers customers in the business's own voice and turns every booking request into an organized lead, with an email to the owner.

Under the hood it runs on modern web tools and Claude, Anthropic's AI. The key decision: instead of reading free text and guessing, the assistant takes structured actions, so every request lands as clean data while the conversation stays natural.

And because the chat is open to the public, I built two layers of protection: a bot check plus a request limit, so abuse can't run up my costs.

It's my fourth build of the same design problem, conversational intake. It's live, fully tested by me end to end, and now running on its first real business site, a brow studio in Florida whose website I also built. Still honestly pre-revenue: I don't claim money I haven't made.

The mobile bug (diagnostic story)

My portfolio assistant worked on desktop and failed on every phone. I worked the problem in steps: checked the connection settings and fixed a real gap I found there, but that wasn't it. A private browser ruled out caching. Then temporary logging showed the server refusing requests: too many.

The cause was my own safety cap. I'd limited each visitor to 40 lifetime messages, and my own phone had used them all up in weeks of testing. The fix was a quiet reset with one retry, so visitors never hit the wall.

Then the honest part: I realized the cap never really protected anything, someone could just reset and keep going on my dime. So I built real protection: a daily limit per visitor, stored privately, and built to fail open so a hiccup never blocks a real visitor.

My own fix showed me a deeper design gap, and I closed it.

Under the hood (for the dev in the room)

The architecture is deliberately boring in the best way: four managed services, each doing one job.

Cloudflare handles DNS and bot protection at the edge. Vercel runs the Next.js app and the API, and serves the widget script from its CDN. Supabase is Postgres plus auth: agent configs, captured leads, with row-level security so every business only sees its own data. And n8n, self-hosted on my own EC2 server, handles the lead notification flow: when the assistant captures a lead, the API fires a webhook toward it.

The design decision I'm proudest of is the embed. A client adds one script tag with a public key, and that's the entire integration. The script figures out the API address from its own source URL, so nothing is hardcoded, and the key fetches that business's setup at runtime. One identical file serves every client with completely different branding and knowledge.

I also designed the failure modes to degrade instead of break. If the notification layer goes down, leads still save to the database: the owner loses an email, never a customer. And anything trust-sensitive, like the booking link, ships as validated data from the server, not text the AI generates, so the AI can't mangle what matters.

I designed and built all of it solo, and verified it end to end in my own testing.

"A decision you're proud of" — the escalation banner

When the concierge hits something it shouldn't answer, like medical, payments, or refunds, it shows the guest a banner: Lonnie has been notified, she'll reach out personally. Early on, that was a lie. Nothing notified her. The chat just ended.

I flagged that as a UX integrity defect, not a missing feature. The interface was making a promise the system wasn't keeping, to the exact guests who were most anxious.

"A mistake" — the Africa birthday trip (deliver slowly)

Mid-project, a client booked a month-long Africa trip for her 30th birthday. She'd planned club nights and a specific beach-club birthday dinner. I designed an elevated version, swapped her plans for a high-end safari, because that's what I assumed a premium trip should look like.

"Working with stakeholders" — the onboarding pivot

Later in the project, Lonnie came to me with a concern. She wanted a real lead process. When I dug into it, she was right in a way the data confirmed. Contact info was being collected at the end of the reservation flow, right before success. So anyone who browsed, got interested, and left never existed to her.

Her instinct became a full front-of-funnel redesign. The AI concierge (conversation design, not engineering)

The concierge was a conversation design problem before it was an engineering problem. I didn't have a research budget, so I treated her inbox as my dataset. I went through what guests actually asked and built a taxonomy: documents, health requirements, money, packing. That became the knowledge base.

Then the design decisions. Suggested-question chips, because first-time users freeze at a blank prompt. Trip-aware voice, so it says "your cruise" on a cruise and knows that trip's specifics.

The hardest part was deciding what it shouldn't answer. When a question needs human judgment, it escalates to the advisor with full context, so the guest never hits a dead end.

Hard guardrail: this concierge is bespoke. Never call it ROSE, never say it was retrofitted. Bespoke buys depth, productized buys reach, and choosing between them is the judgment.
Systems thinking — the IA that flexes

The interesting IA problem was that a four-night cruise and a month-long, three-country journey are completely different user journeys. A cruise is pick a cabin, one ship, fixed dates. The Africa trip is join any leg, choose your hotels, deal with two visa systems.

So I abstracted the part that's the same, which is discover, understand, reserve, track, and let the middle layer flex. Shared components like the hero, countdown, FAQ, and concierge stay constant so the brand never breaks.

Trip-specific pieces swap underneath: cabin cards for cruises, a leg-by-leg timeline for journeys. That's what makes it a product instead of a series of one-off websites.

Same spine: discover, understand, reserve, track → middle layer flexes → product, not one-offs
Honest numbers — the copy rule and the pricing pull

One governing copy rule: never show a fake number, never hide the total, never make a promise the math doesn't support. No fabricated discounts, no hardcoded prices that go stale. Everything is dynamic with an honest fallback if data is missing.

I'd added exact dollar figures for gratuities and travel insurance. I like precision. Lonnie asked me to pull them, because cruise lines update those constantly and a stale exact number is a broken promise waiting to happen.

So we moved to honest approximations with a "Lonnie confirms your rate" handoff. Her domain expertise beat my precision instinct, and the product is more trustworthy for it.

Never print a number the system can't guarantee

And my research was all proxy. The advisor was a rich source since she fields every question, but I'd want direct guest interviews next time. Watching a first-time cruiser actually go through the reservation flow would've caught things faster.

FollowThisModel: the three panel answers

Your payment system never moved real money. Why trust it? I would never claim it works in production, because it hasn't run there. "How do you prioritize?"

One rule runs both levels: I prioritize by the frequency and severity of real user pain, weighed against the effort to fix it. Loudest, most-frequent pain that's cheap to solve goes first. That's a judgment call I can defend with receipts, not a framework I'm reciting.

Across products: prioritizing also means deciding what not to build. I paused Push Coach at a working MVP because it hit a gate I don't control with zero users waiting. I'd rather stop honestly than polish something nobody asked for.

"How do you balance user needs against old backend systems?"

My version of the old-system problem was the website-builder era of 2100. I had the user needs mapped, but the platform simply couldn't deliver them. "How do you use data to improve a live product?"

"How do you present design to non-designers?"

Almost everyone I've ever presented design to was not a designer: security management, a travel advisor, my own guards. Three rules I've learned.

Lead with their problem, not my solution. My manager didn't care about my color choices, he cared that he couldn't read the schedule on shift.

Translate decisions into their stakes: "this extra ID step exists so a careless tap can't fake a safety record" lands with an operations manager in one sentence.

And when my word isn't enough, borrow credibility: I once settled a disagreement by putting the expert on a live call instead of arguing.

Explaining design to non-designers isn't dumbing it down. It's changing the currency: from design language to risk, money, and time.

"Walk us through a complex user journey. How did you handle conflicting constraints?"

The journey I'll walk you through is a security guard's overnight shift in a Dallas high-rise. The interesting part is that my own design principles conflict inside this journey.
