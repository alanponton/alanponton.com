2100 Security

The problem

A five-person overnight security team in a 33-floor Dallas high-rise. The schedule was a sticky note on the key box, then an Excel sheet a guard had to zoom into on his phone at 4 a.m.

Five guards, three posts, 33 floors, overnight.

2100 Security

The user

Guard Reyes was new. He wore glasses, his phone screen was cracked, and he told me the schedule was hard to follow. He also said something I had not planned for.

He wanted to check his post before he left home, not after he clocked in at the desk. That one sentence moved the whole product. It stopped being a thing you look at on shift and became a thing you check from your kitchen at ten at night.

Guard names in this deck are changed. The work is not.

2100 Security

How might we

The first version of the question was wrong. I started with how might we put the schedule online.

The real one was how might we answer one question, what's my post right now, in under five seconds, one-handed, at 4 a.m. Everything after that slid into place, because the second question ranks the whole screen and the first one doesn't.

2100 Security

Three versions

  • V1, paper and Excel. The baseline.
  • V2, a Wix site. My security manager approved it day one.
  • V3, a production PWA I designed and built. She gave me room to build the real one.

Timeline: paper to production PWA.

2100 Security

What V2 taught me

My security manager opened the V2 link and got a browser security warning. I started explaining that the domain forwards to a Wix site that does have HTTPS and a valid certificate.

She said, "Huh?" And she was right.

If the person approving it cannot get past the front door, the design behind the door does not exist. I told her I would come back with something easier to understand. That is why V3 is a real app on its own domain.

2100 Security

Entry with almost nothing

No password. He taps his name, enters his employee ID, and he is in. A password is one more thing to forget at 4 a.m., and forgetting it means the schedule goes back to being a sticky note.

Passwordless entry: employee ID only.

2100 Security

Ranked by urgency

The home screen answers in order of how fast he needs it. Current block first, then his assigned post, then the patrol route under it. Everything else is one tap away, not one scroll away.

2100 Security

Two principles fight

My first principle: zero-friction access beats feature richness, so login is stripped to almost nothing. My second: except where friction saves people.

Once a month he inspects fire extinguishers, and there I deliberately added friction, a second ID confirmation, because a careless tap there creates a safety record that lies. Same app, two opposite calls, both right, because the constraint isn't in the interface, it's in the stakes.

2100 Security

The handoff

Every shift inherits what the last shift saw. Passdown notes live inside the app instead of a notebook at the desk, so the guard reads them where he already is, on the same screen that told him his post.

2100 Security

One tap to Command Center

When something is wrong, nobody should be hunting for a phone number. The Command Center line is a single tap from any screen, because in an emergency the interface should get out of the way completely.

2100 Security

The rules

Every rotation is checked against five written scheduling rules before anything ships. The engine in code runs the nightly rotation and the safety gate. Same input, same answer, whether the rule runs in code or in me.

Fire gate: calendar logic drives the monthly inspection.

2100 Security

Light and dark

I put hours into a version I was proud of. She looked at it and said, "Nah, I don't like it." That landed hard, and I did not let it show.

I took a breath and asked her why. She said, "I can't see it." It was never about taste.

It was contrast, in a lit lobby and a dark stairwell, on a cracked screen at 4 a.m. I built light and dark as equals. She saw the next version and told me it was good work.

Lonnie's Locations

The problem

My client is an independent travel advisor running thirty-plus person group trips out of Chicago. Her business was thriving but everything ran through texts and DMs.

Thirty-plus person group trips, many first-time cruisers.

Lonnie's Locations

How might we

I started with how might we get her trips online. That was a brochure question.

The real one was how might we let a guest answer their own questions, so Lonnie stops being the search engine for thirty people at once. Her time was the constraint, not her web presence.

Lonnie's Locations

The spine

One structure for every trip. Discover, understand, reserve, track. A guest moves through the same four moments whether it is a cruise or a land journey, so nothing has to be relearned trip to trip.

Lonnie's Locations

The middle layer flexes

The spine holds, the middle changes. Cabin cards for cruises, a leg-by-leg timeline for journeys. That flex is what makes this a product instead of a series of one-off websites.

Lonnie's Locations

Choosing a cabin

For a first-time cruiser, picking a cabin is the moment the trip stops being an idea. The flow answers what it costs, what is included, and what happens next, in that order, because that is the order the questions arrive in.

Lonnie's Locations

The pricing pull

I added exact dollar figures for gratuities and travel insurance to the reservation flow. I like precision. Lonnie asked me to pull them, because cruise lines update those prices constantly, and a stale exact number is a broken promise waiting to happen.

Her domain expertise beat my precision instinct. The rule that came out of it governs the whole platform: never print a number the system can't guarantee.

Lonnie's Locations

Copy is a contract

A gold banner in the concierge chat said Lonnie has been notified, she will reach out personally. I was testing as a guest on my phone, one step from sending her the link, and I read my own microcopy the way a guest would. So I asked the question.

Does this system really notify her? It did not. The detection worked, the banner displayed, and nothing behind it existed.

A promise on screen with nothing behind it breaks trust worse than no promise at all. I built the loop: every escalation logged, a live dashboard ranked by urgency, and the agent now asks for the guest's contact info. The banner finally tells the truth.

Caught it myself in testing.

Lonnie's Locations

Capture the person first

Lonnie asked for a lead process. Underneath the ask was a drop-rate problem. I audited the flow and found contact capture sitting at the very end, one screen before success.

Anyone who browsed and left early was invisible, and there were no analytics anywhere to even count them. I moved capture to a conversational welcome, one question per screen, phone optional to protect completion.

The eighteen-question planning form became five. There is no before-and-after number, because no measurement existed before. I fixed a structural blind spot, not a measured funnel.

Lonnie's Locations

The concierge

Her guests ask the same questions at midnight that they ask her at noon. The concierge is built for this platform specifically, on her trips and her policies, which buys depth instead of reach. Choosing custom over a general product was the design judgment, not a technical one.

When Lonnie asked me to strip speculative pricing from its knowledge base for liability, it was the same rule again. Never print a number the system cannot guarantee.

Lonnie's Locations

I designed her out of her own trip

Chloe was planning three weeks in Kenya, Tanzania, and Zanzibar for her 30th. I built an elevated version of her itinerary. It saw thirty, Africa, premium tier, and produced a magazine cover story.

Then I audited my own work against her original document. I had cut her birthday dinner at the beach club. I had cut the Kilimanjaro day hike she had already named the tour operator for.

I had cut the women's beading cooperative, and every club she had listed in Nairobi. She wanted to dance on her birthday and spend a morning with that cooperative. My version treated that as a contradiction and resolved it by deleting half of her.

Lonnie's Locations

Archived, not deleted

Nobody complained. No client flagged it. I caught it because I asked to see what my version had removed before it ever reached her.

The faithful itinerary became the live page, and it kept the same polish, because honoring what she chose should not mean a downgrade. The elevated version is still in the database, unpublished and reversible, in case she ever wants it.

Her expertise adds to the client's vision. It does not overwrite it.

The archived row is still there.

Lonnie's Locations

Behind the counter

She runs the business from the admin side. Traveler lists, trip detail, and the lead flow all live in one place, so the tool she uses daily got the same design attention as the site her guests see.
