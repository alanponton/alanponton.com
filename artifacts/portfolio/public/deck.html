2100 Security

The problem

A five-person overnight security team in a 33-floor Dallas high-rise. The schedule was a sticky note on the key box, then an Excel sheet a guard had to zoom into on his phone at 4 a.m.

Five guards, three posts, 33 floors, overnight.

2100 Security

The user

A tired guard at 4 a.m. abandons anything hard. He taps his name, enters an employee ID, and lands on the one question he actually has. What's my post right now. From there, his full rotation, his patrols, and the notes handed down from last shift.

2100 Security

How might we

The first version of the question was wrong. I started with how might we put the schedule online. The real one was how might we answer one question, what's my post right now, in under five seconds, one-handed, at 4 a.m. Everything after that slid into place, because the second question ranks the whole screen and the first one doesn't.

2100 Security

Three versions

  • V1, paper and Excel. The baseline.
  • V2, a Wix site. My security manager approved it day one.
  • V3, a production PWA I designed and built. She gave me room to build the real one.

Timeline: paper to production PWA.

2100 Security

What V2 taught me

The Wix version worked on my screen and failed on theirs. Guards kept hitting browser security warnings before the schedule ever loaded. Trust broke at the door, so the design behind it never got a chance. That is why V3 is a real app on its own domain.

2100 Security

Entry with almost nothing

No password. He taps his name, enters his employee ID, and he is in. A password is one more thing to forget at 4 a.m., and forgetting it means the schedule goes back to being a sticky note.

Passwordless entry: employee ID only.

2100 Security

Ranked by urgency

The home screen answers in order of how fast he needs it. Current block first, then his assigned post, then the patrol route under it. Everything else is one tap away, not one scroll away.

2100 Security

Two principles fight

My first principle: zero-friction access beats feature richness, so login is stripped to almost nothing. My second: except where friction saves people. Once a month he inspects fire extinguishers, and there I deliberately added friction, a second ID confirmation, because a careless tap there creates a safety record that lies. Same app, two opposite calls, both right, because the constraint isn't in the interface, it's in the stakes.

2100 Security

The handoff

Every shift inherits what the last shift saw. Passdown notes live inside the app instead of a notebook at the desk, so the guard reads them where he already is, on the same screen that told him his post.

2100 Security

One tap to Command Center

When something is wrong, nobody should be hunting for a phone number. The Command Center line is a single tap from any screen, because in an emergency the interface should get out of the way completely.

2100 Security

The rules

Every rotation is checked against five written scheduling rules before anything ships. The engine in code runs the nightly rotation and the safety gate. Same input, same answer, whether the rule runs in code or in me.

Fire gate: calendar logic drives the monthly inspection.

2100 Security

Light and dark

Dark mode shipped early, and not as a style choice. My security manager could not read the schedule on shift. The same screen has to work in a lit lobby and in a dark stairwell, so both are first-class, not one and a toggle.

Lonnie's Locations

The problem

My client is an independent travel advisor running thirty-plus person group trips out of Chicago. Her business was thriving but everything ran through texts and DMs.

Thirty-plus person group trips, many first-time cruisers.

Lonnie's Locations

How might we

I started with how might we get her trips online. That was a brochure question. The real one was how might we let a guest answer their own questions, so Lonnie stops being the search engine for thirty people at once. Her time was the constraint, not her web presence.

Lonnie's Locations

The spine

One structure for every trip. Discover, understand, reserve, track. A guest moves through the same four moments whether it is a cruise or a land journey, so nothing has to be relearned trip to trip.

Lonnie's Locations

The middle layer flexes

The spine holds, the middle changes. Cabin cards for cruises, a leg-by-leg timeline for journeys. That flex is what makes this a product instead of a series of one-off websites.

Lonnie's Locations

Choosing a cabin

For a first-time cruiser, picking a cabin is the moment the trip stops being an idea. The flow answers what it costs, what is included, and what happens next, in that order, because that is the order the questions arrive in.

Lonnie's Locations

The pricing pull

I added exact dollar figures for gratuities and travel insurance to the reservation flow. I like precision. Lonnie asked me to pull them, because cruise lines update those prices constantly, and a stale exact number is a broken promise waiting to happen. Her domain expertise beat my precision instinct. The rule that came out of it governs the whole platform: never print a number the system can't guarantee.

Lonnie's Locations

Copy is a contract

A banner promised something the product did not deliver yet. That is not a missing feature, it is an integrity defect, and it costs more than the feature would have. Every promise in the copy now has something behind it.

Lonnie's Locations

Capture the person first

Lonnie felt it before the data showed it. Guests were being asked to commit before anyone knew who they were. We moved the capture earlier, so the relationship starts before the reservation does.

Lonnie's Locations

The concierge

Her guests ask the same questions at midnight that they ask her at noon. The concierge is built for this platform specifically, on her trips and her policies, which buys depth instead of reach. Choosing custom over a general product was the design judgment, not a technical one.

Lonnie's Locations

Behind the counter

She runs the business from the admin side. Traveler lists, trip detail, and the lead flow all live in one place, so the tool she uses daily got the same design attention as the site her guests see.

Lonnie's Locations

Results

  • Live in production. Three trips, thirty-plus guests served.
  • Roughly $2.50 a month in infrastructure. One designer.
  • A signup through the site became a new group trip she's organizing.

December 2025 to present. Solo build.
